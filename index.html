About

A little about me

I am broadly interested in the design and analysis of efficient algorithms, particularly in settings where exact solutions are computationally intractable. My work sits at the intersection of combinatorial optimization, linear programming, and the theoretical limits of algorithmic approximation.

I completed my BS–MS Dual Degree in Mathematics at the Indian Institute of Science Education and Research (IISER), Pune, where my master's thesis — supervised by Prof. Amit Kumar and Prof. Naveen Garg at IIT Delhi — focused on the Submodular Interval Stabbing Problem. I will be joining TU Dortmund as a PhD researcher, where I plan to continue working on structural questions in approximation and optimization.

Outside research, I enjoy mathematical exposition — I have given talks on topics ranging from Turing's foundational work to greedy algorithms and matroids, and organized proof-based competitions at IISER Pune's math club.

Research

What I work on

My research centers on approximation algorithms — the study of how well we can efficiently solve optimization problems that are NP-hard in the worst case. I am particularly drawn to approaches grounded in linear programming relaxations and the geometric structure of polytopes, and to understanding where such methods provably reach their limits.

1
Approximation Algorithms & LP Rounding
Designing efficient algorithms with provable approximation guarantees using LP relaxations, rounding schemes, and dual-fitting arguments.
2
Submodular Optimization
Algorithmic and hardness questions for problems involving submodular objectives, including generalizations of classical covering and packing problems.
3
Limits of LP-based Approximation
Understanding when LP relaxations cannot yield good approximations — integrality gaps, APX-hardness reductions, and inapproximability results.
4
Structural Graph Theory & Combinatorics
Investigating graph-theoretic parameters (asymmetric coloring numbers, comparability graphs) and their connections to algorithmic problems.
